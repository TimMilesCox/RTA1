2.	a change in EXIT$

	a thread other than process-initial thread
	exits simply

	if it exits of its own accord it removes
	itself from the initial thread's chain

	if STOP_FLAG is < zero it the thread exits
	without trying to access the owner chain.
	The owner TCB may be already be reassigned

	this is the case where the thread is target
	of broadcast STOP$. Deletion from the owner
	chain has happened already

	if it's an initial thread with no other
	threads it also exits simply

	initial thread owning threads stops all
	threads in its thread list serially

	outA	STOP$PORT

	it should not do that to itself or it
	will wind the kernel up. The initial thread
	is not on its own threads chain

	the initial thread can then continue exit_ii
	immediately, because no thread will
	subsequently touch its resources

	a thread exiting and finding its STOP_WORD
	negative will not try to remove itself from
	the initial thread's chain because the
	initial thread has done that and may not be
	there, depending of the cause of the deletion

	there shall also be a system call for killing
	a thread or process. Where it is a thread
	it comes out the owner thread chain before
	the STOP$ broadcast

5.	the initial thread directly owns all other threads
	of the process. They are in its thread list
	This hierarchy has a depth of 1. Threads can
	start threads, but they don't own them

	The initial thread has a a zero owner-pointer
	and may or may not have a list of threads

	subsidiary threads have an owner pointer to the
	initial thread. On EXIT$ they come out of the
	list in the initial thread

6	a similar interrupt broadcast can be added to
	suspend threads, since it's also not known whether
	they are candidates for switching or actually
	running in another instruction processor

	in the case of a suspend the ISR of the broadcast
	interrupt flags the thread descriptor, not the
	TCB, and if the thread is running in an instruction
	processor it then goes to yield_ii
